Preparation

Description:

This step outlines everything you need to accomplish before embarking on your home search. This includes identifying your preferences for a new home, gathering all necessary documents for the journey ahead, and establishing a realistic budget. By preparing thoroughly in advance, you can approach the process with confidence, ensuring you are comfortable with your decisions and grounded in your expectations for your new home. This proactive approach not only puts you in control of your choices but also streamlines the entire process, making it as efficient as possible. While unexpected changes or exigent circumstances may arise, the following steps are designed to account for the anticipated course of action. Good luck!

Wants & Needs

Description: The journey begins by articulating your vision for your new home. The most effective approach is to create a comprehensive list categorizing must-haves, needs, and wants. Must-haves encompass elements that are indispensable to you, such as location, parking spaces, and proximity to public transportation. Needs are similar but represent features you might be open to adding later, such as large kitchen islands or a specific number of bathrooms. Wants are additional aspects that enhance the appeal of a house when comparing different options.

This step is incredibly beneficial as it allows you to streamline your house search with your realtor. It also enables you, at a later stage, to compare your choices and factor in any potential renovation costs associated with a particular property.

Remember! There are a lot of surprise costs that you may incur in the first year of your new home including any unexpected repairs, general upkeep, insurance costs. It is smart to set aside additional funds for these as well!

Create Your Budget

Description: When crafting a budget, remember to think SMART: Specific, Measurable, Attainable, Realistic, and Time-bound! Ensure that your budget accounts for explicit costs, including fixed expenses (such as mortgage, down payment, health care, and credit cards), maintenance costs for your new home (covering home repairs, heating, and electricity), expenses related to the home-buying process (such as payments to realtors and lawyers), as well as discretionary costs like food, clothing, and daily expenditures.

This approach is crucial for establishing realistic expectations as you progress to defining your preferences for a new home. It enables you to distinguish between wants and needs based on specific data points, facilitating a more informed decision-making process.

Something that may be helpful: write these down and begin a folder for all the documents that you will eventually need to gather for buying your home!

Gather Documents

Description: When you are meeting with lenders make sure to bring:

  1. Identity: your driver’s license, passport, state approved id
  1. Social Security Card
  1. Tax returns: all tax fetus and W-2s from the past two years so the lender can verify if household income was consistent
    1. If you are self-employed instead of W-2s bring your 1099 forms for the same reason
  1. Pay Stubs from the past 30 days
  1. Bank Account statements from, the last 60 days
    1. Make sure to bring statements from ALL accounts: checking, savings, investments, and retirement (for investment and retirement quarterly is fine)
  1. Additional documents: these are based on exigent circumstances
    1. Any divorce documents
    1. Any debt or bankruptcy related documents
    1. Any rental history
      1. The best way to judge this is if it is related to financial history, bring it! It is always better to bring more than less

It helps to make a folder with all these documents and keep it organized so you are never rushing and have all these documents in one place. There is a lot of paperwork so organization is key to reducing stress!

Build Your Team

Description:

Now that you have an idea of a budget and what you are looking for in your new home it’s time to assemble your team! This includes a lender and a realtor/ real estate agent.

To find a reputable lender, seek recommendations from family and friends who have previously purchased homes, and consult with the BHC (Boston Home Center). Additionally, the attached section includes a list of approved lenders affiliated with the BHC and other governmental agencies (In order to qualify for any financial assistance programs you HAVE TO work with lenders on this list). You can also utilize the NMLS database to verify a lender's certification to operate in Massachusetts.

Similarly, hiring a real estate agent (licensed and passed a listening exam) or a realtor (part of the National Association of Realtors) can provide you with more houses that are now available to the public yet as well as help streamline your house hunting process and make it more efficient.

Finding an agent may seem tricky here are some things you can do to find the perfect one for you:

  1. Use your network of friends and family to see who they may have used to find their home
  1. Do your own research: figure out if you want to work with a broker firm or an independent agent, look at reviews, do they have a good reputation
  1. You can use realtor.com to look for NAR licensed agents in your area

Choose Your Home

Description:

Selecting your home is the fun part! Walk-through different houses with your real estate agent, take notes of which you like, what you like etc. It may be helpful to make a pros and cons list (or equivalent) so you can later discuss and compare everything you have looked at with ease. Once you have looked through and found your home it is time to put in an offer which you have discussed with your agent. At this point the seller can agree or provide you with a counter-offer— you can of course, agree to that or deny and then continue looking.

Before submitting an offer it is important to take another look at your budget factoring in all closing costs: any repairs that need to be done? what do commuting costs look like? general utility costs? This is important so that when you are walking in to your new home you are not ambushed by any surprise expenses you did not realize before. Additionally, keep in mind costs of home inspections, home insurance, property taxes, and if applicable any Homeowner Association (HOA) fees.

Hire An Attorney

Description:

Once you and the seller agree, you will make a deposit and the home will be in escrow— meaning you are contractually expected to buy the house under the assumption there are no serious damages to the home you were unaware of (NOT costs you did not account for). Typically the lender you use will have a closing attorney but you are free to hire your own as well.

At this time, you may want to discuss with the BHA about hiring an attorney to draw up an agreement prior to accepting the offer. This is to provide you with additional security related to price, closing date, pre-approval letter and overall reviewing the contract for your new home!

Finalize your Mortgage

Description:

Now that you have accepted an offer it is time to also go back to your lender with additional documents to have your mortgage finalized. These documents include:

  1. Contract to purchase: the signed purchase and sale agreement. If there is any seller credit for closing costs, this must be included
  1. Verification of employment (if requested)
  1. Rental History: This is to prove you have a history of paying on time
  1. Proof of Down payment and Closing Funds
  1. Any other documents that may explain exigent circumstances

During this stage, your lender or loan specialist will let you know when you are ready to buy homeowner’s insurance. Make sure you are able to afford this because if not, the lender will force insurance on your home which will be MORE expensive. Additionally, keep in mind your lender may require you to pay 10-20% of your annual home insurance premium upfront so it is crucial to have some additional funds set aside for this.

Add all of these to the folder you may have created as well!

Inspection & Appraisal

Description:

There are two official things left to do: Get your home appraised as well to double check the loan is accurate to the value of the home.

And, even if your home looks perfect, it is crucial to have the home inspected. This will make sure that the home is in fact flawless and there is no serious or structural damage that was not disclosed to you. A home inspection general includes: roof, visible insulation, HVAC, interior plumbing and electrical systems, attic, windows and doors, foundation, walls, floors, and ceilings.

If there is certain damage that was not disclosed you are able to rescind your offer and get the deposit back or work with the seller to make repairs or discount the price.

If there is none, well you are all set, your home is ALMOST yours!

An appraiser generally looks at: comparable sales, square footage, appearance, amenities, and overall condition

Closing Disclosure

Description:

Almost there!

Other documents you should have been asked to sign include:

  1. Settlement statement: summarizes all final charges and fees
  1. The promissory note: legal note showcasing summary of all payments, when they should be paid, and where the should be sent
  1. Deed of mortgage or trust: The document in which you sign a lien (security deposit of sorts) as security for repayment of your loan

This is where you will also pay all closing costs so make sure you have enough funds to be able to cover those by this meeting… Congratulations!!
